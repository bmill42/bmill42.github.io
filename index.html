Music, computation, style

I am a scholar specializing in the recent history of music theory, particularly the postwar reception of ideas from information theory and cybernetics as exemplified in Leonard Meyer's theory of musical style. In my dissertation, this historical work leads to a close engagement with, and critique of, recent practices in data-driven computational music theory, also known as corpus studies. Some of my recent and forthcoming publications consider topics ranging from the semiotics of musical Markov models to robots that play jazz.

More generally, I'm interested in a broad range of humanistic thought as it intersects with music and sound, including media theory and the philosophy of technology, sound studies, and music of the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries. I also have an interest in Adorno's philosophy of music, and have written on his treatment of the concept of style in his little-studied Towards a Theory of Musical Reproduction. I'm a co-convenor of the working group on Sound and Technology sponsored by the Consortium for the History of Science, Technology, and Medicine.

I'm currently a lecturer in the Program for Computing in the Arts and Sciences at the University of Michigan, where I teach classes on critical computing and computing for creative expression. I've previously worked as a data scientist at the Institute for Social Research at the University of Michigan. I completed my PhD in music theory at Yale in 2020, where I've also taught as a lecturer, and I have undergraduate and masters degrees in computer engineering and music theory, respectively, from the University of Kansas. Outside of my work, I particularly like tennis and greyhounds. This website is something of a work in progress, but you can find information about academic publications and presentations, along with writings and code related to ongoing personal projects.
